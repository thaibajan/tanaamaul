Ideation session

Personalised health based on your biochemistry: the preventative approach

I'm deep into health and wellbeing — it's one of those things that's woven into everything I do. Over the last few years I've worked closely with health coaches and practitioners, and I've seen the same pattern play out over and over: someone gets extensive blood work done, receives a PDF of 100+ biomarkers, and has absolutely no idea what to do with any of it.

At the same time, I've been deep-diving into Claude and its many different features. I realised it pretty much 10x's the entire UX and product development lifecycle. What used to take me days of wireframing and iteration took a fraction of the time. So I used real use cases from what I've observed — typical client lifecycles, how check-ins happen, where the gaps are — and mocked up a concept.

My objective was simple: design a use case where technology enhances the customer's journey using AI, rather than what I see a lot of right now — AI being used to create distance between the customer and the practitioner, purely to boost margins, while the actual experience goes down the drain. I believe the future is preventative medicine, and there's so much scope to get this right.

Backstory

Why I believe preventative health is the future

It started during the pandemic, when there was a bit of a wellness boom. I noticed people I'd gone to school with — who'd gone into conventional medicine — starting to specialise in more preventative, root-cause approaches. Around the same time, I discovered Dr Mark Hyman, one of the founders of Function Health, and what he was saying about health was stuff I'd genuinely never heard before. I went deep — his podcast, his books, the audiobooks — and it became a kind of North Star for how I thought about personal wellbeing.

What I liked was the precision-based approach. When you go down the natural health path, it can sometimes feel a bit fluffy — good intentions but not always the clearest explanations. And with conventional medicine, you get the rigour but it's often reactive rather than preventative. The best practitioners I've encountered combine both: evidence-based reasoning alongside a holistic, root-cause angle. Best of both worlds. And that's what motivated this project — how do you take that quality of thinking and make it accessible through technology, without losing the nuance?

Process

How Claude changed the way I build

Claude was instrumental from the very start — not just in the design work, but in the ideation. I used it to brainstorm different concepts, weigh up which idea to work on that day, and explore angles I hadn't considered. The general user flow was established fairly early on because I had a very clear problem in mind, but Claude helped me think through the details much faster than I would have alone.

For the actual mockups, it was a back-and-forth process. Claude would help me generate wireframe ideas, but it took a lot of customisation and iteration to get things where I wanted them. I didn't want it to look like something Lovable spat out in five seconds. This was a couple of months ago — before Figma MCP was even a thing — so what I ended up doing was exporting from Claude as HTML and using a Figma-to-HTML plugin to bring it into Figma, which I thought was brilliant. That let me refine the UI in a way that felt more personalised and intentional. Then I'd bring it back into Claude, back into Figma, and keep iterating.

The whole thing reinforced something I've been thinking a lot about lately: AI doesn't replace the craft, it accelerates it. You still need taste, you still need to know what good looks like, and you still need to push past the first output. But the speed at which you can move from idea to something tangible is genuinely different now.

Beyond the core screens, I also added a habit tracking section and the ability to book a session with your health coach — who would of course be at a much lower rate than an actual doctor. I thought that was a really clever way to build accountability into the whole experience. Here's what you know you need to do. Here's the interface that helps keep you accountable. And here's a real human who helps keep you accountable — all on demand. Combine that with the insights into each marker, the patterns they tell as a group, and a clear roadmap showing what to focus on in the right order, and you've genuinely given the user the power to stay on a consistent health journey. I thought that was really powerful.

In terms of the UI itself, I used Tailwind UI as a starting point but made it look slightly different from what I was seeing out there — calm, cool colours and a serif font paired with a sans-serif, which gives it a clean, clinical feel without going crazy design-wise. If I were to actually build this, I'd spend more time dialling in the design. But this was really just about doing something for fun and seeing how far I could push the concept in a short space of time.

01

Start with what matters today

Most health apps dump you into a wall of data. I wanted the home screen to feel more like a coach checking in — here's your adherence this week, here's what your body's been doing, here's the one thing to focus on next.

Cronometer's UI was a huge inspiration here — it's an app I use daily to track my own macros, and I love the micronutrient features. But what I realised is it becomes really powerful when you can combine that nutritional data with your actual biochemistry. And then if you add supplement tracking into the mix, you've got the full picture: your nutrition, your supplements, and your blood work — all correlated, feeding into a continued roadmap so you're always working towards a specific goal.

Because the reality is, health is fairly overwhelming. There are a million different things you could do that will move the needle. You need to know what they are and in what order. That's where it gives true power back to the user.

02

Two-minute check-ins that your practitioner actually reads

This was the bit I was most interested in. Having worked alongside practitioners, I've seen how client check-ins actually happen — it's WhatsApp messages, voice notes, the odd email. Information gets lost, context gets missed between consultations, and the practitioner ends up asking the same questions every call.

I designed a structured weekly check-in — one question per screen, dial ratings for severity, multi-select for symptoms. Takes about two minutes. The practitioner gets clean, structured data they can review before a call instead of scrolling through a chat thread. Technology enhancing the relationship, not replacing it.

03

Protocols are useless if you can't stick to them

Anyone who's been on a preventative health programme knows the supplement stack alone can be overwhelming — zinc bisglycinate before bed, raw ginger before meals, specific proteins at specific times. Most people fall off within a fortnight.

I kept the habit tracker deliberately simple. Weekly grid, streak count, category filters. No gamification guilt trips. Just quiet checkmarks that accumulate. The kind of thing that makes you think "yeah, I can do another week."

04

The right thing at the wrong time can do more harm than good

This is where most health apps completely fall apart. They give you everything at once — 30 recommendations, 12 supplements, a meal plan — and expect you to figure out sequencing on your own. But in preventative health, order matters. If someone has leaky gut and you throw a parasitic cleanse at them at the same time, you can trigger endotoxemia — circulating even more toxins through the bloodstream when the body hasn't had a chance to recover. Doing the right intervention in the wrong phase can genuinely set someone back.

That's why the roadmap is phasic. One phase at a time, in the right order, according to best practice. Rebuild the foundations first — digestion, nutrient absorption, daily rhythm — before you even think about optimisation. The "20% that gets 80% of results" section surfaces the highest-leverage habits at the top so people know exactly where to start. And a one-tap coach booking because sometimes you just need someone qualified to talk it through.

05

Patterns, not isolated numbers

I've had extensive blood work done myself, so this one was personal. What I've realised about preventative health is that what actually works is looking at biomarkers not one at a time, but through the lens of patterns and correlations — and having someone qualified and certified interpret them with the aim of longevity, not just the avoidance of disease. What's classed as "normal" testosterone today, for example, is historically very low compared to our ancestors. There's no single source of truth, but a lot of people do heal from this approach when the data is read properly.

I grouped everything into body systems — heart, metabolic, hormonal, liver, immune — with a traffic-light status on each. But the real value is in the connections between them. Elevated fasting glucose alongside raised HbA1c and HOMA-IR tells a different story than any one of those markers alone. Drill in and you get plain-language explanations backed by the latest research, optimised for longevity rather than just "within range." There's so much power in making that accessible.
